Introduction

Background

Job seekers, particularly individuals in the midst of career transitions, grapple with significant mental health challenges throughout the job-seeking process. Extensive prior research has substantiated the profound impact of unemployment, job searches, and career changes on individuals’ well-being, which includes escalating stress levels, diminishing self-esteem, depressive feelings, and pervasive loneliness, among other emotional burdens.
“It has been pretty challenging to have to apply yourself daily, when you can’t help but want to sit and moan about your frustrations, I want something that I am able to act to get myself out of the slump.” – client interview
Our team is dedicated to crafting a product equipped with actionable features designed to empower job seekers to manage their mental health on a day-to-day basis.

Research Question

❓
How do we develop a tailored mental health support platform for job seekers, addressing their unique emotional challenges and fostering their overall well-being, confidence, and resilience during the hiring process?

Goals

In this first phase of product building, the primary goal of UX research was to explore the problem space and identify an overall product strategy. Specifically, our goals are:
To understand job seekers’ demands and pain points, including mental health challenges, coping methods they have used, and the unmet needs they currently face with existing products or solutions.
To define the scope of our product based on achieving the first goal, ensuring that our solution aligns with their requirements and aspirations.

Challenges in Data Cleaning

Upon collecting survey data, I realized a significant obstacle to data analysis is the format of multiple-choice questions. Exported from Google Forms, the responses to multiple-choice questions are stored as lists—for each respondent, all selected options are grouped together, using commas as separations. This prevents us from analyzing the results of each option. There were 5 multiple-choice questions in total, so manually decoding these questions would be taxing.
ALT

My Contribution: Automating Data Cleaning with Python

To solve this problem, I leveraged Python to develop a function capable of handling all multiple-choice questions in our survey. The goal was to automatically generate dummy variables for each option of all five questions.
The function examines each row of the original column and checks if it contains an element from the list of all options. When a match is found, the function assigns a value of 1 to the corresponding new column, and this process repeats until all entries in a row have been examined.

def multichoice_to_variables(df, column_name, all_choices, prefix, inplace=False): """This function create extra columns corresponding to the choices of a multiple-choice question. Columns are named by prefix_ and thier original choice. Return the modified dataframe. Entries for 'Other' will be printed. """ if not inplace: df = df.copy() for i in all_choices: df[f'{prefix}_{i}'] = 0 df[f'{prefix}_other'] = 0 for i in range(len(df)): resp = df[column_name].iloc[i] resp = resp.lower() while resp: for j in all_choices: if resp.startswith(j.lower()): df.at[i, f'{prefix}_{j}'] = 1 resp = resp[len(j):] resp = resp.lstrip(', ') break else: df.at[i, f'{prefix}_other'] = 1 print(i, resp) break return df

Findings & Impacts

Insights from User Survey Results

We first conducted descriptive analysis on all questions, which provided valuable insights into how users engage with job search and mental health apps. Here are some key findings:
Prevalence of Mental Health Issues: More than 50% of the participants described their mental state as "surviving," "struggling," or "in crisis," rather than "feeling decent" or "thriving."
 Job Seeker Reliance on Job Search Apps: Over 80% of respondents reported that a substantial portion of their job search activities occur on job search apps.
Limited Usage of Mental Health Apps: In contrast to the widespread use of job search apps, mental health apps are less frequently utilized. A significant portion (69%) of respondents stated that they do not use any mental health apps. Among those who do use such apps, the majority (82.8%) use them less than once a month.
User Preferences for Types of App: When asked to express their preference between "a job application app designed around maintaining mental health" and "a mental health support app for job seekers," more than half (57.9%) of respondents favored the former choice.
ALT

Impact on Product Strategy

Motivated a significant shift in our product strategy. Initially, the strategy team focused on exploring potential mental health features, including mood checking and community building. However, the research results led us to pivot our strategy towards building a job search app that is more conscientious of mental health concerns.
Incorporated “goal tracking” for job search as a central feature for consideration. As shown in our research, many respondents highlighted the absence of features in current job-seeking and mental health apps, including "Goal setting and tracking," "Job application behavior tracking," "Reminders of goals," and "Streak/milestone tracker". This finding was corroborated by another survey question, where 84% of respondents rated the goal-tracking feature as extremely or somewhat useful for career development. I brought the importance of goal tracking to the XFN team, which formed the cornerstone for refining our product strategy.

Impact on Design

To gain deeper insights from the survey results and address specific feature questions from the design team, we explored factors associated with job seekers’ mental health well-being. Some key findings and sample visuals are as follows:
Tech Industry Experience: Whether people have prior experience in the tech industry is associated with their mental health status. A higher proportion of individuals with tech experience reported "thriving" or "feeling decent" compared to those without such experience.
Total Job Search Duration: Job search duration also showed a correlation with mental health status. Individuals searching for jobs for less than six months were more likely to report "thriving" or "feeling decent."
Daily App Usage Hours: Although most users spent less than two hours a day on job search apps, there was still an observable association between app usage hours and mental health status. A larger proportion of users who spent less than two hours on these apps reported "thriving" or "feeling decent" compared to those who spent more than two hours.

Reflection and Next Steps

We conducted the survey within the communities where job seekers want to pursue a product-related job in the tech industry. This sample represents early-phase professionals and may overrepresent women, as shown in the survey data. We need further discussions on how the representativeness of our sampled data could impact our product strategy.
In the next phase of research, the team can conduct user interviews, diary studies, and other qualitative methods to gain a deeper understanding of what “goal tracking” means to users. These methods will help uncover best practices for goal tracking and how these practices contribute to job seekers' mental well-being. The qualitative approaches will provide valuable insights for understanding user experience and refining our product.
